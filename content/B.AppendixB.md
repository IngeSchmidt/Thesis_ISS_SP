# Interview with Pedagogue and Teacher
| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| To what extent does interaction through movement contribute to understanding the corresponding material? | Magic acts as a kind of trigger for interest. Because students are amazed, their curiosity is stimulated and attention is temporarily increased. A piano is easy to follow for upper secondary students, because it is familiar to them. Working with something unfamiliar can act as a trigger for interest. It will lead to some confusion, but this actually helps motivation. The challenge is to explain it in such a way that they start to understand it. This is the responsibility of the teacher. If you explain things step by step, you can teach a lot. However, the steps should not be too large, as that can be demotivating and cause people to drop out. This is called the Zone of Proximal Development by psychologist Lev Vygotsky. |
| Is “magic” in a contactless detection method of added educational value, or does it lead to confusion and lack of clarity? | Images and interactions help a lot here. They extend the attention span. For an average person, attention can be maintained for about 20 minutes. This can be extended by not only presenting information but also asking questions. It also helps to show a video, and even more so to physically place the material in the classroom and let students interact with it themselves. Seeing what happens always works better than trying to explain it with words alone. |
| Does it add educational value if the device detects movement in three dimensions instead of one dimension? | This makes it feel magical, but it is harder to follow. You should not make the step too big in order to stay within the Zone of Proximal Development. When teaching, you need to know the students’ starting level. You must estimate how far you can go in one lesson. Everything you do not explain is better left out, otherwise the “magic” remains unclear. If students still do not understand at the end, it feels like a loss for them, even if the learning goal was achieved. Ending a lesson by saying that the next lesson will cover the sensing gives a good starting point for the following session. |
| We also want to add a visualization feature to the device so students can see what the sound waveform looks like. Do you think that, in addition to the waveform, it would also be valuable to show the Fourier transform and a spectrogram? | Visual representations are very effective for understanding. A screen would be ideal to show what students are doing with their hands. It directly shows what the waves should look like and how this affects sound, connecting all three representations. |
| Is it valuable to let the device generate not only a sine wave but also other waveforms, such as a square wave or sawtooth wave? | It can be interesting to show multiple things, but it depends on how much time you have. In a 45-minute lesson, you should not try to include too much. Anything you cannot fully explain should be left out. This could be used in a separate lesson. It may work well with a strong class, but it should not be included in introductory lessons. You could make it an optional feature that is not always visible, to avoid overwhelming students with information they are not ready to process. |
| Do you have any suggestions for our device to further increase student enthusiasm or improve understanding of the physical principles? | The additional visualizations and explainations could be useful for follow-up lessions or for students who quickly understand the initial concepts.. It allows for differentiation and keeps advanced students engaged as well. |
